Email spam trigger words are specific words and phrases that cause spam filters (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo) to flag your email and send it to the junk folder instead of the inbox. Common examples include "free," "act now," "guaranteed," "click here," and "make money." This guide lists 600+ of them organized by category, with risk levels and safer alternatives.

One wrong word in your subject line and your cold email goes straight to spam. Not because your offer is bad. Because spam filters are pattern-matching machines, and they flag specific words before a human ever sees your message.

How Spam Filters Actually Work in 2026

Modern spam filters look at three things: content, sender reputation, and engagement history. Content filtering is where trigger words matter. Filters like SpamAssassin assign point values to suspicious words. Hit a threshold and your email is flagged.

But context matters. Using "free" once in a paragraph is different from a subject line that reads "FREE MONEY NOW." Filters weigh subject line words 2-3x heavier than body words. ALL CAPS multiplies the risk. And stacking multiple trigger words in one sentence is the fastest way to the spam folder.

The words below are organized by category. Each table shows the trigger word, its risk level (high/medium/low), and a safer alternative you can swap in.

How to Write Emails That Avoid Spam Filters

Avoiding trigger words is step one. Here's how to keep your emails out of spam consistently:

1. Keep your subject line clean

Use 4-7 words. No ALL CAPS. No excessive punctuation. Write it like you'd write to a colleague, not a billboard. Check our guide on ideal email length for sales outreach for more on this.

2. Write like a human

If your email reads like a marketing brochure, it will be treated like one. Short sentences. Plain language. One ask per email.

3. Watch your sender reputation

Trigger words matter more when your sender reputation is low. If you're new to cold email, warm up your domain first. High bounce rates tank your reputation fast. Read more about why emails bounce and how to fix it.

4. Test before you send

Check your email with our free Spam Checker to catch trigger words and spam score issues before they cost you deliverability.

5. Don't stack triggers

One medium-risk word in a well-written email probably won't hurt you. Three trigger words in the same sentence will. Filters look at density, not just presence. Review the full list of 500 trigger words for reference.

6. Personalize your emails

Generic mass emails get flagged. Emails that reference the recipient's company, role, or recent activity look legitimate because they are. This is why cold email done right works while spam does not.

The Bottom Line

Frequently Asked Questions

What are email spam trigger words?

Email spam trigger words are specific words and phrases that cause spam filters to flag your email as junk. Filters like SpamAssassin assign point values to these words. When the combined score exceeds a threshold, the email is routed to spam instead of the inbox. Common examples include "free," "guaranteed," "act now," and "click here."

How many spam trigger words should I avoid?

There is no fixed number. A single trigger word in an otherwise well-written email from a reputable sender usually won't cause problems. The risk comes from stacking multiple trigger words in the same email, especially in the subject line. As a rule: avoid high-risk words entirely, and limit medium-risk words to one per email.

Does using the word "free" automatically send my email to spam?

Not automatically. Spam filters evaluate context, not just individual words. "Free" in a subject line combined with ALL CAPS and exclamation marks will almost certainly get flagged. "Free" used once in the body of a personalized email from a warmed-up domain will probably be fine. The risk multiplies when "free" appears alongside other trigger words.

What is the difference between spam trigger words in the subject line vs. the body?

Subject line words carry 2-3x more weight in spam scoring than body text. A word like "urgent" in your subject line is far more likely to trigger filters than the same word buried in a paragraph. This is why your subject line needs to be the cleanest part of your email.

Do spam filters work the same across Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo?

No. Each provider runs its own filtering system. Gmail relies heavily on engagement signals (opens, replies, clicks). Outlook weighs sender reputation and authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC). Yahoo uses a mix of content analysis and IP reputation. The trigger words in this list are flagged across all major providers, but the weight each provider gives them varies.

Can I check my email for spam trigger words before sending?

Yes. Use a spam checker tool to scan your subject line and body text for trigger words before you hit send. These tools show you which words are risky and suggest alternatives. Overloop's free spam checker does this automatically for every campaign.

Are spam trigger words the only reason emails go to spam?

No. Spam filters look at three main signals: content (trigger words), sender reputation (domain age, authentication, bounce rates), and engagement (do recipients open, reply, or mark as spam). Trigger words matter most when your sender reputation is new or weak. A strong sender reputation can offset some content risk, but stacking trigger words will hurt even established senders.
